    • Ramen

    By: Maycaela Enguito

    Ramen is a Japanese dish that has taken the world by storm in recent years. Originally a simple noodle soup, ramen has evolved into a complex and diverse culinary experience that has captured the hearts and taste buds of people all over the world.

    Ramen was first introduced to Japan in the late 1800s by Chinese immigrants. The dish was initially called "shina soba" or "Chinese soba" and was a simple noodle soup made with wheat noodles in a soy sauce or salt-based broth. Over time, the recipe evolved and other ingredients such as pork, vegetables, and eggs were added.

    After World War II, ramen became a popular street food in Japan, with vendors setting up stalls and carts to serve the dish to hungry customers. In the 1950s, instant ramen was invented by Momofuku Ando, the founder of Nissin Foods. Instant ramen became a staple food in Japan and eventually made its way to other parts of the world.

    The key ingredients in ramen are the noodles and the broth. The noodles are made from wheat flour, salt, and water, and are typically thin and curly. The broth is the heart of the dish and can be made with a variety of ingredients such as pork bones, chicken, seafood, or vegetables. The broth is simmered for several hours to extract the flavor from the ingredients and create a rich and flavorful base.

    Other ingredients commonly found in ramen include chashu (braised pork), menma (fermented bamboo shoots), narutomaki (fish cake), green onions, and nori (dried seaweed). Some variations of ramen also include corn, butter, or even cheese.

    Making ramen from scratch is a time-consuming process that requires patience and skill. The broth is typically simmered for several hours to develop the flavor, and the noodles are made fresh and cooked just before serving. The toppings are prepared separately and added to the bowl just before serving.

    Instant ramen, on the other hand, is a quick and convenient option that can be prepared in just a few minutes. The noodles are precooked and dried, and the broth is in the form of a powder or a liquid concentrate. Instant ramen is a popular choice for college students and people on the go, but it is not as flavorful or nutritious as homemade ramen.

    There are many variations of ramen, each with its own unique flavor and style. Some popular variations include shoyu ramen (soy sauce-based broth), miso ramen (miso-based broth), tonkotsu ramen (pork bone-based broth), and tsukemen (dipping noodles). Regional variations of ramen can also be found throughout Japan, with each region having its own unique twist on the classic dish.

    Ramen is a delicious and satisfying dish that has captured the hearts and taste buds of people all over the world. Whether you prefer a traditional bowl of shoyu ramen or a modern twist on the classic dish, there is a ramen for every taste and preference. While homemade ramen may take time and skill to prepare, the end result is a flavorful and nutritious meal that is worth the effort. So why not grab a bowl of ramen and enjoy the rich and complex flavors of this beloved soup.
